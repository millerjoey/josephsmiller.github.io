Coverage and coresets with kernel entropy

Suppose I can keep only $N$ points. I might want those points to cover as many distinct possibilities as possible, or I might want them to approximate a particular population. Those sound similar, but they are different optimization problems. A single similarity-sensitive entropy makes the distinction precise.

One entropy, two questions

I previously motivated similarity-sensitive entropy and derived its associated tangent scoring rule. I will use the same entropy here without rederiving typicality:

$$H_K(q)=-\mathbb E_{X\sim q}\log (Kq)(X).$$

For this post, the outcome space is the real line and similarity is fixed in advance by a one-dimensional Laplace kernel,

$$K_\ell(x,y)=\exp\!\left(-\frac{|x-y|}{\ell}\right).$$

The scale $\ell$ says which numerical differences matter. This choice is useful for more than its simple interpretation: $H_K$ is concave for the one-dimensional Laplace pullback class. That makes the tangent loss below proper. A Gaussian RBF can still be useful as a coverage kernel, but it does not give the same general concavity guarantee.

Coverage is target-free, not mass-free

The clean coverage problem has no external population distribution. The design $q$ is itself the object we choose:

$$q_{\mathrm{cov}}\in \arg\max_{q:\,|\operatorname{supp}(q)|\le N} H_K(q).$$

So coverage is not literally distribution-free: $H_K$ always acts on a distribution. It is target-free. There is no separate law $p$ whose prevalence the selected points are supposed to reproduce.

If I will run each selected design exactly once, the mass is fixed by that experimental constraint: $q=u_S$ is uniform on a set $S$ of size $N$. Then the problem is

$$S_{\mathrm{cov}}\in\arg\max_{|S|=N}H_K(u_S).$$

If repeated allocation is allowed, the weights should be optimized too. Equal mass is therefore not part of the definition of coverage. It represents the special, common design in which each retained point receives one unit of attention.

The coreset version has a target

Now suppose I observe $M$ points $x_1,\ldots,x_M$. Their empirical law is explicit:

$$p_M=\frac1M\sum_{i=1}^M\delta_{x_i}.$$

A size-$N$ coreset is a sparse weighted law supported on observed points,

$$q_{S,w}=\sum_{a=1}^N w_a\delta_{z_a}, \qquad z_a\in\{x_1,\ldots,x_M\}, \qquad w\in\Delta_N.$$

Here the weights are not design effort. They say how much population mass each retained atom represents. The natural loss is the regret of reporting $q$ when the target is $p_M$:

$$\begin{aligned} C_K(p,q)&=H_K(q)+\nabla H_K(q)\!\cdot\!(p-q),\\ D_K(p,q)&=C_K(p,q)-H_K(p),\\ (S_{\mathrm{core}},w_{\mathrm{core}}) &\in\arg\min_{|S|\le N,\,w\in\Delta_N}D_K(p_M,q_{S,w}). \end{aligned}$$

Concavity of $H_K$ gives $D_K(p,q)\ge 0$. This makes the coreset objective a loss with a real reference point: zero means that $q$ is as good as the target itself under the kernel scoring rule.

The same points give different answers

Consider 20 observations on the line: 13 fill the interval from $-1.5$ to $-0.3$, five fill $0.45$ to $0.85$, and two sit at $1.65$ and $1.75$. Set $\ell=0.30$ and keep four atoms.

Neither answer dominates the other. Coverage deliberately ignores the observed frequencies and asks for an even allocation across distinctions. The coreset deliberately preserves those frequencies. Asking the coverage solution to match prevalence would quietly turn it into the coreset problem.

Why ordinary entropy cannot do this

Coverage Coreset
External target None $p_M$
Objective Maximize $H_K(q)$ Minimize $D_K(p_M,q)$
Meaning of mass Allocation of attention Population mass represented
Identity-kernel failure Every uniform $N$-point set has entropy $\log N$ Every strict sparse approximation has infinite ordinary cross-entropy

The two failures are almost opposites. Shannon entropy cannot distinguish the geometry of two equally sized subsets, while ordinary cross-entropy refuses to compare a discrete coreset with mass it omits. The Laplace kernel fixes both by making nearby atoms partial substitutes at the declared scale $\ell$.

Takeaway

Coverage asks: how should I distribute $N$ units of attention across distinct possibilities? A coreset asks: how well can $N$ weighted atoms reproduce this target distribution? Both can use the same kernel entropy, but only the second has an external target.

This leaves one important modeling choice visible rather than hidden: the scale $\ell$. If it is tiny, near-points stop substituting for one another and the ordinary pathologies return. If it is huge, meaningful distinctions disappear. Here I assume that scale is supplied by the task; learning it is a separate problem.
