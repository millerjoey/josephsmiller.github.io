Coverage and coresets with kernel entropy

Selecting $N$ points from a sample can be useful as either a data-coverage problem or a coreset problem. In coverage, an equally weighted subset is chosen to minimize redundancy under the kernel. In coreset construction, a sparse weighted distribution approximates a specified target. I want to show how both objectives can be formulated using basic concepts derived from similarity-sensitive entropy.

Kernel entropy and bandwidth

I previously described similarity-sensitive entropy and derived its associated tangent scoring rule. The entropy is

$$H_K(q)=-\mathbb E_{X\sim q}\log (Kq)(X).$$

For this post, the outcome space is the real line and similarity is fixed in advance by a one-dimensional Laplace kernel,

$$K_\ell(x,y)=\exp\!\left(-\frac{|x-y|}{\ell}\right).$$

The scale $\ell$ determines which numerical differences are treated as similar. For the one-dimensional Laplace pullback class, $H_K$ is concave, so the tangent loss below is proper.

Coverage objective

Let $\mathcal X_M=\{x_1,\ldots,x_M\}$ be the candidate points. Sampling $N$ points without repeats gives a set $S\subseteq\mathcal X_M$ with $|S|=N$ and the uniform distribution

$$u_S=\frac1N\sum_{z\in S}\delta_z.$$

The coverage objective is

$$S_{\mathrm{cov}}\in \arg\max_{S\subseteq\mathcal X_M:\,|S|=N}H_K(u_S).$$

The set constraint excludes exact repeats. The entropy then distinguishes among those subsets: kernel-similar selections increase one another's local similarity and reduce $H_K(u_S)$. There is no external target distribution, and the weights are fixed at $1/N$ rather than optimized.

Coreset objective

Suppose I observe $M$ points $x_1,\ldots,x_M$, with empirical law

$$p_M=\frac1M\sum_{i=1}^M\delta_{x_i}.$$

A size-$N$ coreset is a sparse weighted law supported on observed points,

$$q_{S,w}=\sum_{a=1}^N w_a\delta_{z_a}, \qquad z_a\in\{x_1,\ldots,x_M\}, \qquad w\in\Delta_N.$$

Here the weights represent population mass rather than design effort. The coreset loss is the regret of reporting $q$ when the target is $p_M$:

$$\begin{aligned} C_K(p,q)&=H_K(q)+\nabla H_K(q)\!\cdot\!(p-q),\\ D_K(p,q)&=C_K(p,q)-H_K(p),\\ (S_{\mathrm{core}},w_{\mathrm{core}}) &\in\arg\min_{|S|\le N,\,w\in\Delta_N}D_K(p_M,q_{S,w}). \end{aligned}$$
  • $q$ is the sparse model reported for the target $p_M$.
  • $D_K(p_M,q)$ is its regret under the kernel scoring rule.
  • Concavity of $H_K$ gives $D_K(p,q)\ge 0$.
  • $D_K(p,q)=0$ means no loss relative to reporting the target.

Argument outline

  • Coverage. Maximize $H_K(u_S)$ over equally weighted $N$-point subsets; there is no external target.
  • Ordinary-entropy limit. With $K=I$, every uniform $N$-point subset has entropy $\log N$; subset geometry disappears.
  • Coreset. Treat the sparse weighted law $q$ as a model for $p_M$.
  • Ordinary cross-entropy. If $q$ omits any target atom, its loss is infinite.
  • Common workaround. Introduce a task-specific discrepancy, feature map, query class, moment criterion, or other compression objective.
  • Kernel model. $K$ makes nearby atoms partial substitutes, so omitted atoms can still receive finite predictive similarity.
  • Scoring interpretation. The coreset objective is the regret $D_K(p_M,q)$ induced by $H_K$, rather than a separate compression heuristic.
  • Main claim to develop. Coreset construction becomes model selection under a proper similarity-aware scoring rule.

Example

  • Target: 20 observations; group sizes $13$, $5$, and $2$.
  • Support budget: $N=4$.
  • Reference bandwidth: $\ell=0.30$.
  • Coverage: equal weights; maximize $H_K(u_S)$.
  • Coreset: optimized weights; minimize $D_K(p_M,q_{S,w})$.
